Replay is the feature that quietly changes how you build. When history is
retained and cursors are cheap, "reprocess everything since Tuesday" stops
being a war-room project and becomes a function call.

## Three replays you'll actually do

### 1. Bootstrap a new service from history

Your team ships a search service eight months after orders started flowing.
With a consumed-and-gone queue, you'd write a custom backfill against the
orders table, keep it consistent with live traffic, and pray during the
cutover. With Vulkan:

```go
vulkan.Subscribe(client, "orders", "search-indexer", indexOrder,
    vulkan.FromOffset(0))
```

The group starts at the oldest retained event, chews through history at its
own pace (its lag shrinking is your progress bar), and seamlessly arrives at
live traffic. Other groups never notice it happened.

### 2. Recover from a bad deploy

Tuesday 09:14: a deploy ships a bug in `warehouse-sync` that silently
mis-handles EU orders. Wednesday: you notice. The fix:

<Steps>

1. Deploy the corrected handler.

2. Rewind the group to before the damage:

   ```go
   client.Rewind(ctx, "orders", "warehouse-sync",
       vulkan.ToTimestamp(time.Date(2026, 6, 9, 9, 0, 0, 0, time.UTC)))
   ```

3. Watch it re-run the window. Handlers are idempotent (they already had to
   be — [at-least-once delivery](/concepts/lifecycle/)), so re-processing
   the messages it had handled correctly is harmless.

</Steps>

No data archaeology, no hand-written SQL repair scripts at midnight, no
"which orders did we miss?" spreadsheet. The log knows.

### 3. Rebuild a projection from scratch

Event-sourcing-style read models — a dashboard table, a denormalized cache, a
materialized aggregate — can be **disposable** when the events are durable.
Schema change in the projection? Drop the table, rewind the group, rebuild:

```go
client.Rewind(ctx, "orders", "revenue-dashboard", vulkan.ToOffset(0))
```

<Aside type="tip" title="Replay is a consumer-side decision">
Notice that none of these required touching producers, other consumers, or
stream configuration. A cursor is private state; moving yours is nobody
else's business. This is the deep reason fan-out and replay come from the
same design decision — [retention](/concepts/streams/).
</Aside>

## Dry runs (Cloud)

Rewinding a group that fires emails is exciting in the wrong way. Vulkan
Cloud's replay console shows exactly which events a rewind would redeliver —
count, time range, sample payloads — before you commit, and can target the
replay at a **shadow group** wired to a no-op or staging handler first.

## What replay costs

Nothing at write time — it's the same append-only log either way. At replay
time it's sequential indexed reads, the cheapest thing Postgres does.
The real budget question is **retention**: how much history to keep
(`vulkan.Retain(30*24*time.Hour)`), traded against storage. Thirty days of
JSONB events is usually laughably small next to the rest of your database —
keep more than you think you need.
