A picture-first intro

The Reading Bookmark

How a message platform remembers how far it has gotten — even when work finishes out of order, and even when one item is hopelessly broken.

Imagine reading a long, numbered book. You read mostly front-to-back, but sometimes you skip ahead and read a later page early. How do you remember your true progress — the point past which everything is read?

The bookmark answers that question. It sits at the boundary where all earlier pages are read. The green sticky tabs mark later pages you finished early — ahead of the bookmark.

In our system the "book" is a durable, append-only message log: messages numbered 0, 1, 2, 3 … and never reordered. A team of workers "reads" (processes) them. The bookmark-plus-tabs idea is what we call the managed cursor.

The cursor has two parts

A single number, plus a small scrapbook of exceptions.

1 · The Bookmark

One integer: the highest page N such that every page up to N is finished. A bookmark means "all prior pages read" — so it can only move forward when there are no gaps behind it.

Everything behind the bookmark collapses into just this one number and is thrown away. Cheap forever.

2 · The Read-Ahead Tabs

A little scrapbook of pages above the bookmark whose outcomes are mixed: some read early (tabs), some still being read, some set aside to re-read, some torn out.

This scrapbook only ever holds the messy frontier — its size tracks how out-of-order you got, not the length of the whole book.

The slide (and why it can leap)

When the pages between the bookmark and a run of tabs all become finished, the bookmark slides forward, sweeping up those tabs and collapsing them into the integer. Filling a single gap can let it jump several pages at once — that's the cascade.

Where this metaphor leaks

No picture is the territory. Here's the honest ledger.

What it makes vivid ✓

  • Why the bookmark must be contiguous. A bookmark inherently claims "everything before is read" — so a gap below it is nonsensical.
  • Why one bad item can't freeze you. A skip slip on a torn page lets the bookmark glide past — no head-of-line blocking.
  • Why the slide can leap. Filling the one page between bookmark and a strip of tabs sweeps them all up at once.
  • Why the scrapbook stays small. It only holds the messy frontier; finished pages collapse into the integer and are discarded.

What it hides / distorts ✕

  • One reader. Really there are many workers, on many machines, claiming distinct pages with no coordination. A lone reader badly understates the concurrency.
  • The bookmark isn't on the hot path. Workers never touch it; a separate background pass slides it lazily. The book makes it feel like you move it as you read.
  • Torn pages are kept, not gone. "Torn out" suggests discarded — dead messages are actually retained in a dead-letter store for audit.
  • Crash behavior & at-least-once. Per-page outcomes are durable; a lost slide just recomputes. A page can also be read twice. The cozy book hides all of this.
  • No real "re-reading order." Retries can land anytime; the calm "set aside for later" understates the churn of a busy frontier.
