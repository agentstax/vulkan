The Managed Cursor · The Shipping Line

How a message platform stays caught up even when one parcel jams the line, the gate keeps moving.

A durable log hands out numbered messages forever. Workers finish them out of order. We need a cheap, crash-safe way to know “everything up to here is done” without one stuck message freezing the world. Picture it as a shipping line.

01 The cast on the line

Every piece of the metaphor maps to one real thing. Keep these in mind; the rest is just watching them work.

The belt
append-only log
Numbered parcels (offsets) arrive in order and never change.
The seal gate
the mark (high-water)
Seals one contiguous finished run from the front. Can’t pass a parcel still being worked.
The packers
competing consumers
Many workers, even on different stations, grab parcels out of order and work them independently (short leases).
The reject chute
dead-letter store
A damaged parcel is dropped aside (kept for audit) so the gate can advance past it.
The supervisor
background roll-up
Walks the line now and then and slides the gate over the finished run. Packers never touch the gate.

02 Reading the parcels

Each parcel’s colour is its state. Colour is never the only signal — every chip has a glyph and a label too.

05 Where this metaphor leaks

Every model lies a little. Here’s the honest accounting for the shipping line.

✓ What it makes vivid

  • Head-of-line blocking is physical. Finished parcels visibly pile up behind one jam — you see why the mark is stuck.
  • The reject chute earns its keep. Dropping a dead parcel aside so the gate advances is the whole pitch in one image.
  • Roll-up is clearly someone else’s job. A separate supervisor advancing the gate makes the lazy, off-hot-path nature obvious.
  • The cascade is satisfying. One parcel clears and the gate leaps several slots — the jump is unmistakable.

✕ What it hides or distorts

  • A belt implies one neat queue. Real packers may live on different machines pulling the same log with zero shared belt — there’s no physical line.
  • Parcels don’t “move toward” a gate. The mark is a number that’s recomputed; nothing physically travels. Position here is just offset order.
  • Leases & timeouts are barely shown. A “short hold” glosses over crash-recovery, lease expiry, and the at-least-once (possible double-processing) guarantee.
  • The chute looks lossy. “Reject” suggests trash; dead-letters are fully retained, queryable records — not garbage.
  • A supervisor sounds like one boss. Roll-up can be concurrent/idempotent and crash mid-walk; a stale-low gate is fine and self-heals — the single-walker image undersells that.
